Typography, Three Combinations

A: Cormorant + DM Sans. Hairline serif. Legal brief written by engineers. Very editorial, delicate at small sizes, commanding at large ones.
B: Playfair Display + DM Sans. Sturdier serif. Newspaper authority. More legible below 32px. Less fragile on mobile.
C: League Spartan + Montserrat. Full geometric sans. No serifs, no italics. Bold, compressed, modern. Reads as a product company that takes compliance seriously, not a law firm. The accent quote becomes bold + tracked in this mode.

Page Section Background Rhythm

Sections alternate between --bg-1 (white card surface) and --bg-0 (warm off-white page base). The footer is always --bg-1. Two rules enforce visual cadence:

1. The last content section before the footer must be --bg-0, so the footer reads as its own zone rather than a continuation of the page.

2. Adjacent sections never share the same background. If a new section lands on the same bg as its neighbor, flip it.

Each section also carries a borderTop: 1px solid var(--br-subtle) to sharpen the boundary even when backgrounds already contrast.

Why alternate and not a single background? A uniform page background makes long pages feel like one undifferentiated scroll. Alternating --bg-0 / --bg-1 creates natural section breaks without heavyweight dividers. The color shift is subtle enough to stay quiet but obvious enough to signal "this is a new topic."

Why must the pre-footer section be --bg-0? The footer uses --bg-1 by definition (it holds the logo and nav links, which need the clean surface). If the last content section also uses --bg-1, they merge: the footer's border-top is the only separator, and the CTA copy visually floats in what reads as dead white space. --bg-0 before the footer means the footer arrives as a distinct plane.

Why start with --bg-1? The NavBar is also --bg-1 with a border-bottom. Starting the first section with --bg-1 keeps the nav and the hero in the same visual register (same surface, just separated by a line). Starting with --bg-0 would make the hero feel like it's on a lower layer than the nav.

Design Rationale v0.3

Dark teal fix. The v0.2 dark mode teal (#22D3EE) was essentially browser-link cyan against near-black, which creates the same visual jar as unvisited links on a black page. The fix was not to "soften" in an abstract sense but to match the perceptual depth ratio: in light mode, the teal is roughly 40% lightness against a near-white background. The dark mode equivalent at that depth is around 58% lightness against near-black. That lands at #4AB5C4. Same hue, same authority, no neon.

Font C: League Spartan + Montserrat. League Spartan is compressed, heavy, and geometric. It reads like a warning label or a product spec sheet, which is exactly right for a compliance infrastructure company. Montserrat as the body font keeps the same geometric family without the weight. The pairing says "we built a serious product and we know what we are doing." No serifs, no italics, no ornament. If A or B feel too refined or editorial, C is the alternative. If C feels too product-y and not enough institutional, A is the one to commit to.

No italic in Font C. Italic is a serif convention. It reads as emphasis in documents and legal text, which fits A and B. In a geometric sans like League Spartan, italic is just slanted and loses the boldness that makes the font work. The accent quote in Font C mode renders as bold + slightly tracked instead. Still distinguished from body copy. More commanding than decorative.

Headline structure. Line 1: "Move fast." Line 2: "and ~~break things~~ get investigated." Strikethrough covers the phrase as a unit. The original cliché crossed out, the truth remaining. Anyone who knows the original Zuckerberg aphorism reads the joke in one beat. Everyone else still lands on "get investigated" as the point.
